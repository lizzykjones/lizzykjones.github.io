Spain
800 km on foot across northern Spain
▾

800 km on foot across northern Spain — the Camino de Santiago is less a walk than a slow shedding, a daily negotiation between the body and the will. I started in Saint-Jean-Pied-de-Port with blisters by day two and a deep suspicion that I had no idea what I was doing. I was right, and it didn't matter.

Switzerland
Alpine passes and mountain villages
▾

After the Camino, Switzerland felt like landing on another planet — clean, quiet, precise, expensive. I spent most of my time in the mountains, walking trails that were almost insultingly well-maintained compared to the dirt tracks I'd just finished, wondering what it says about a country that its hiking signs have their own typeface.

Turkey
Ancient cities, the Bosphorus, and Cappadocia
▾

Istanbul is a city that refuses to be summarized — ancient and loud and layered, where Europe ends and Asia begins in the middle of a strait. I stayed longer than planned, which is the truest thing I can say about it. Cappadocia, a week later, looked exactly like the photos and somehow still managed to surprise me.

The Cascades
Volcanoes, glaciers, and old-growth forest
▾

The Cascades are not gentle. Glaciers, active volcanoes, wildflower meadows at elevation, old-growth forest that makes you feel small in the right way — the kind of landscape that makes you question why you ever live anywhere else. A brief return to the US, mid-year, which felt stranger than any of the countries I'd been visiting.

Nepal
Diwali, solo trekking, and narrowly escaping a storm
▾

I finally arrived in Nepal, ready to start the journey I had dreamed of for ten years. After three days of flying — exhausted and ready for a real bed — the multicolored flashing lights of the city below, disorganized and neon, twinkling in the dark, felt like a personal welcome. It turned out I had landed during Diwali, which, as the locals say, is for "Hindus, Buddhists, and tourists" alike.

Malaysia
Rainforests, street food, and the South China Sea
▾

Kuala Lumpur operates at a pace that feels simultaneously chaotic and functional — towers and street hawkers and colonial buildings and rainforest, all stacked on top of each other. The food alone is worth the trip: Indian, Chinese, Malay, and everything in between, available at 2am from a cart on the sidewalk.

Korea
Incredible food, study culture, and a society organized around exams
▾

Seoul is organized around excellence in a way that's visible from the street — study cafes full at midnight, hagwons running past dinnertime, the collective pressure that produces both extraordinary achievement and extraordinary burnout. I ate my way through the city and thought about it a lot.

Japan
Temples, trains, and the art of slowing down
▾

Japan rewards slowness. The more I tried to see, the less I actually experienced; the more I wandered without an itinerary, the more the country opened up. Kyoto at 6am, before the tourists arrive, is as quiet as a held breath — and then the crowds come, and it becomes something else entirely, and somehow that's fine too.

Vietnam
Karst mountains, overnight buses, and writing my thesis at 2am in a hostel
▾

Vietnam is long and loud and beautiful, and I loved almost all of it — including the overnight buses, which I loved significantly less than the rest. Ha Long Bay in the early morning fog, Hội An at night, Hanoi's Old Quarter on a borrowed bicycle: it is a country that accumulates experiences faster than you can process them.

Hawaii
The culture shock of coming back to America, mid-trip
▾

After months in Asia, landing in Hawaii felt like the world had been turned down three notches. Familiar chain restaurants, English everywhere, no scramble for a SIM card. The specific strangeness of returning to America in the middle of a year abroad — familiar enough to feel like home, unfamiliar enough to feel like a question.

China
Jiuzhaigou, yak hot pot, chopsticks, and the Wall
▾

In the first week, I discovered I was not as good at using chopsticks as I thought I was. I managed to spill grease on every single item of clothing I brought, developed a new skill for removing grease stains, and by the end had progressed to a 1-in-3 success rate — though if it's hot pot, forget it.

Taiwan
A lost passport, boba, and a very unexpected hiking encounter
▾

Taiwan is the place that surprised me most. I went with low expectations and left wondering why I hadn't come sooner — great food, genuinely kind people, mountains that appear without warning at the edge of the city, and the best boba I've had anywhere. Also: a lost passport, a minor bureaucratic adventure, and a hiking encounter I still can't fully explain.

Cameroon
Six weeks teaching physics and rethinking what it means to make a difference
▾

I arrived in Cameroon after a flight that failed to land on its first attempt, diverted to a different city, then took off again five hours later. Six weeks followed: teaching Newton's Laws with bottle rockets, cold bucket showers, bucket laundry, extraordinary fruit, and a lot of things I didn't expect to reckon with. Worth every second.
