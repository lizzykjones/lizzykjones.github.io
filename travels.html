Camino de Santiago
Spain
A Frankenstein route with two friends, on approximately three hours of sleep
▾

I showed up for the Camino de Santiago on roughly three hours of sleep — coming off college graduation, a conference talk on my senior thesis, and dropping my things in Geneva, all in the same week. My friends Sophia and Taya were gracious enough not to mention this. We did a hybrid route: the coastal Camino del Norte in the north, then rented a car and joined the last 100km of the Camino Francés into Santiago. Blisters by day two, certificates by the end.

A summer at the world's largest particle collider
▾

After the Camino I went to Geneva to spend the summer as a student at CERN, which had been a goal of mine since high school. The Large Hadron Collider is every bit as impressive as advertised. The cost of lunch outside the campus cafeteria is also impressive, in a less good way.

Turkey
Istanbul, a hammam, bedbugs, and Fethiye — a forced Schengen exit
▾

My Schengen Zone visa was running out before I needed to be back in France, so I did what any reasonable person does and searched Google Flights for the cheapest way out of the EU. That turned out to be Istanbul. I spent a week there — mosques, architecture, a hammam, some very committed soccer fans, and bedbugs — then headed south to Fethiye for a week of hiking Saklikent Gorge and sitting on a beach.

The Cascades
A last-minute solo, a flagship REI garage sale, and five nights in the mountains
▾

I was back in the States for a bit and joined my dad on his Seattle layover on the way to Japan. First stop was the flagship REI, where I found a brand new tent at the garage sale. Then I solo hiked Section J of the PCT — Stevens Pass to Snoqualmie Pass — five nights, ~20-mile days, and more cool PCT trekkers per square mile than anywhere I've been.

Nepal
Diwali, solo trekking, and narrowly escaping a storm
▾

I finished my GRE and left for Nepal the next day, which in retrospect was an interesting sequencing. I arrived during Diwali — not planned, just lucky — which in practice meant landing in a festival, getting spectacularly lost in a tarp-covered market, a security guard cutting an entire SIM card line on my behalf, and a motorbike taxi whose knees touched the neighboring motorbikes at every stop. The Annapurna Circuit followed: still one of the best treks I've done, and I narrowly avoided getting caught in an early snowstorm at altitude.

Malaysia
A two-day layover that accidentally became a real trip
▾

I went to Kuala Lumpur because it was the cheapest layover on the way to Korea and I had two days to fill. The hostel had a guitar in the common room, which were my main criteria. The food was a mix of Chinese and Islamic influences and was excellent. The botanical gardens were free. Two days was nowhere near enough — I left with a longer list of things I hadn't seen than things I had.

Korea
A 5am airport pickup, hanboks in Jeonju, and a society organized around one exam
▾

My friend Rori's dad drove three hours to the airport before 6am to pick me up, which I still think about. She, her dad, and his wife took me on a road trip to Jeonju, where we rented traditional hanboks, explored a recreated historical village, and had bibimbap with kimchi — which I was surprised to find I actually liked, since I don't usually like pickled things. Korea was the most culturally homogeneous place I'd ever been, which I was not expecting to notice as much as I did.

Japan
Grad school apps, an onsen hotel, and missing peak maple season by one week
▾

I met my dad in Tokyo in late November — a significant upgrade from the previous month of cheap, communal hostels where I hadn't stayed in the same place more than three nights in a row. We had a hotel, dinner options, and someone else to split things with. I spent most of the trip in a café working on graduate school applications, which were due December 15. For the weekend we went to a mountain town with natural hot springs, stayed in a traditional onsen hotel, and missed peak maple season by about a week. The ground was carpeted in leaves.

Vietnam
Karst mountains, overnight buses, and writing my thesis at 2am in a hostel
▾

The hostel was $3 a night, and the bed was hard enough that I eventually just inflated my camping mat on top of it. I worked on my physics thesis at 2am on multiple occasions while partygoers cycled in and out of the karaoke bar next door — nothing like orbital mechanics at midnight to the sound of a particularly uninspired Sweet Caroline. The karsts and caving were genuinely beautiful. Sapa is known for its mountain views and hiking, but it was so completely fogged in the entire time I was there that I couldn't see anything, so I got a foot massage instead.

Jiuzhaigou, yak hot pot, chopsticks, and the Wall
▾

I discovered in the first week that I was not as good at chopsticks as I thought — every item of clothing I brought has a grease stain to prove it. Beijing, which I'd been told was boring, was not boring.

Taiwan
A lost passport, a lost pair of glasses, Alex Honnold, and the best boba I've ever had
▾

I lost both my passport and my glasses in the same 24 hours, immediately after my cousin Kathleen left — a strong start to solo Taiwan. Alex Honnold happened to be free-soloing Taipei 101 that week, which I watched from the ground. The food had a Japanese influence and significantly less grease than China, which I was ready for. I tried fresh taro boba for the first time and it has since ruined all other boba for me.

Cameroon
Four weeks teaching physics and rethinking what it means to make a difference
▾

The flight diverted to a different city and sat on the tarmac for five hours before trying again — a reasonable preview of the four weeks that followed. I taught Newton's Laws using bottle rockets, took cold bucket showers, ate extraordinary fresh fruit, and thought harder about what it actually means to help a place than I had before or since. On the last night I fell into an open sewer trench at 2am getting off the bus and had to take a cold bucket shower at the hotel at midnight. The hotel lunch the next day had maggots in it. A lot happened.
