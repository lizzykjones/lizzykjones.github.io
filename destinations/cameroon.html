Gap Year ’25–26

Cameroon

Six weeks teaching physics and rethinking what it means to make a difference

← All Travels

A longer write-up than most — partly because I spent more time here, and partly because the place left me with more to say.

I arrived in Cameroon in early February, after a flight that failed to land on its first attempt, diverted to a different city, then took off again five hours later. I finally touched down in Yaoundé, the capital. My first surprise was the cost: far more expensive than anywhere I'd been recently in East Asia, with few tourist amenities to show for it. There isn't much of a tourism economy here, so hotels and airport taxis are priced for a small expatriate market rather than backpackers. I was lucky that Nikolas Hall and his Peace Corps cohort were passing through, so I got an early read on life in Cameroon before heading upcountry.

I arrived in Mbouda the following day — a town in the French-speaking west, right along the border of the English-speaking northwest region, which is caught in an ongoing separatist conflict. Less than 150 miles from Yaoundé, the journey takes seven hours. Buses here work like giant shared taxis, picking people up and dropping them off at random points along the road. It was especially interesting to watch the climate change gradually throughout the route.

The house had electricity about half the time, plumbing, and running water for three nights total during my stay. Cold bucket showers. Bucket laundry. You get the picture. That didn't stop us from cooking. With the other volunteers, we made American chili for the Super Bowl (my cornbread attempt failed), homemade cookies — the first the Cameroonian staff had ever tried — and, predictably, a lot of Tex Mex. I also ventured into Cameroonian cooking, which I'll get to.

Teaching

With almost no advance notice — I had to ask to find out I was teaching; in true Cameroon fashion the communication is, let's say, aspirational — I found myself running full two-hour lessons each day. I decided to teach Newton's Laws. First week: the first and second laws, with a bottle-car building competition to explore friction. Second week: pressurized bottle rockets to illustrate Newton's third law, culminating in a school-wide competition. Between 20 and 40 students per class, plus whoever was pressed against the barred windows from outside.

The kids were enthusiastic, full of questions, genuinely into the engineering challenges. Once, some boys slipped out to play hacky sack during a test rocket launch and got caught by the school's discipline master. He marched them to the front of the class and whipped their hands until several of them cried. I had half a mind to intervene. I didn't. This is standard protocol here, and I was a guest in that school. I still think about it.

Food

You can take the girl out of Texas, but you cannot take Texas out of the girl. Limes. Avocados. Black beans. Homemade refried beans. Homemade tortillas. One of the first times I haven't been homesick for Tex Mex while traveling — it turns out the climate in this part of Cameroon is surprisingly similar to parts of Mexico and Central America.

But don't let my enthusiasm for comfort food overshadow the local produce. The fruits here are extraordinary: fresh pineapple so good it rewrites everything I thought I knew about pineapple; mountain strawberries picked from bushes on the side of trails; passion fruit free for the taking; mango season just about to start; hibiscus juice sold iced by kids on the street, tart and perfect. Plantains everywhere. Peanuts everywhere. Cameroonian cuisine itself is rich: ndolé (a bitter-leaf stew), huckleberry and okra leaves, cassava in various forms, spicy sauces, grilled fish and chicken. And then there's deux oeufs spaghetti — two eggs and spaghetti, the breakfast of champions.

Religion

Cameroon is predominantly Christian, with a Muslim minority concentrated mostly in the north. In my region it was overwhelmingly so — I woke most Sunday mornings to enthusiastic singing from the Pentecostal church across the street. There's an interesting split: Pentecostal evangelicalism is more common among francophones, while the Presbyterian tradition came via British missionaries and is stronger in anglophone areas. I attended a Presbyterian service during Lent — same Bible, same structure, same hymns I grew up with in Texas. They had a four-part choir that rehearsed twice a week and used a do-re-mi solfege system to read music. We sang the same songs at essentially the same pace on opposite sides of the planet.

Being La Blanche

Most places I travel, people can tell I'm a tourist at a glance. Here, I'm also white — la blanche — and this fact is announced to me constantly. People call out from across the street, from passing motorbikes, from market stalls. Once, while waiting for a taxi, a group of guys approached with a snare drum, formed a semicircle around me, and performed an improvised "La Blanche" song on the spot. In retrospect, genuinely funny.

Not all of it is. I'll be direct: I have never before felt physically unsafe while traveling solo as a woman. Here I did. I was physically grabbed most times I went out alone. Men followed me after I told them clearly to stop. I received marriage proposals daily. Men felt the need to run alongside me while I jogged, headphones in, clearly not interested. Going outside to buy tomatoes from the stand up the road required budgeting energy for the gauntlet.

I want to be careful to note that this isn't just a "tourist thing." Gender inequality is real and pervasive for every woman here. Women cook, clean, raise children, and work the fields — which is considered women's work, while men cut timber and spend afternoons in the bar. Marriage is transactional; men pay a dowry. At a planning meeting for International Women's Day at a women's rights association, women expressed fear of being too assertive, stated that their place was beneath men because the Bible said so, and said that if they were beaten it was for making the man angry — and there was general consensus around the room. I sat through that meeting with a lot of feelings. I've since found myself impatient with certain conversations at home that now seem comparatively minor. I know that's complicated to say, and I hold it lightly. But the contrast is real.

The Journey Back

Eight hours by bus. Sewer trenches line the sides of the streets in Yaoundé, and at 2am, stepping off the bus, I fell into one. I rode the rest of the way in a taxi reeking of it. Cold bucket shower. The hotel served lunch with maggots in it the next day; my adventurousness with food has genuinely expanded over this year, but I could not clear that bar.

Worth every second.
