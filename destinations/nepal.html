Gap Year ’25–26

Nepal

Diwali, solo trekking, and narrowly escaping a storm

← All Travels

I finally arrived in Nepal, ready to start the journey I had dreamed of for ten years. After three days of flying — exhausted and ready for a real bed — the multicolored flashing lights of the city below, disorganized and neon, twinkling in the dark, felt like a personal welcome.

It turns out that by sheer coincidence I had arrived during Diwali, the Hindu festival of lights. As I learned later, though, this holiday does not discriminate by religion — as the locals said, it is for "Hindus, Buddhists, and tourists" alike. I was soon to discover that Nepalis tend to jump at any chance for a festival, regardless of tradition.

What an incredible blessing to land at this particular time. I was told the city came even more alive at night during the festival, but after my first afternoon of settling in, that was hard to imagine — what with the regular things like getting lost in a tarp-covered market and ending up on the other side of the city, being sent on a wild goose chase to get passport photos from a random shack for my trekking permit, asking a security guard to help me find a SIM card and accidentally leading her to cut the entire line, taking a motorbike taxi on roads so crowded my knees touched the neighbor motorbikes when we stopped. After several weeks sitting at home studying for the GRE, the city was shockingly dense: so alive, so loud, so many smells and colors.

And yet, despite this overwhelming affront on the senses, there was an entirely different feeling from other places I've traveled that are similarly dense and largely tourist-economy. So often I find myself hounded in markets — obviously a tourist, traveling solo, an easy target — people shoving souvenirs in my face or calling after me. Despite walking several miles lost in a market where I didn't see another tourist, nobody so much as glanced at me. It was a very welcome change. I sensed immediately a feeling of relaxedness and happiness in the country, even though its people have less than in places where people have been more pushy. They were genuinely kind — not just wanting my money.
